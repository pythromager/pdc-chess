Piece Disability Chess (PDC) – Rules
Core Principle
All standard chess rules apply unless explicitly overridden below. This includes castling, en passant, promotion, stalemate, and insufficient material draws.
Default rule: No piece may capture, except as specified per piece below.

Piece Rules
Pawn
Standard pawn movement applies. May only capture other pawns (standard diagonal capture) — with one exception: a pawn may also capture an enemy knight if that knight is in “the river” (c4-f5; 8 squares). The pawn must still be diagonally adjacent to the knight to capture it.
Rook
Must capture if any capture is available within range. Capturing range is limited to 3 squares — the rook cannot capture a piece more than 3 squares away even along an open file or rank. The rook may still move beyond 3 squares without capturing. Any piece within the path blocks further captures as normal.
Knight
Can optionally capture any piece that is currently giving check to your king — and only those pieces. No other captures are permitted. The knight may also block check by moving between the checking piece and the king, as in standard chess. Neither of these is forced.
Bishop
The bishop cannot capture any piece. If the bishop can legally move to a square that gives check, it must do so. This obligation applies whenever the bishop has at least one such move available.
If your bishop must give check but cannot — because your king is in check and the king must move instead — that is checkmate, unless the M1 Special Rule saves you.
Note: A discovered check created by another piece moving and unmasking a bishop does not trigger the must-check obligation. The bishop itself must have the option to move and deliver check.
Queen
The queen may capture any piece. It must capture any piece it attacks, except knights — the queen has no obligation to capture knights, though it may do so freely.
Each non-knight enemy piece the queen attacks is its own separate must-obligation. If the queen must capture a piece but cannot — because the king is in check and the king must move instead — that is checkmate, unless the M1 Special Rule saves you.
King
While in check: the king must move. It cannot block or capture the checking piece by any means. This counts as one must-obligation.
While not in check: the king moves and captures normally.

Loss Conditions
Double Must = Loss
If a player has two or more simultaneous must-obligations that cannot all be satisfied in a single move, that player loses immediately — unless the M1 Special Rule applies.
Every individual obligation counts as its own separate must:
A queen attacking two pawns = 2 musts
A queen attacking two pawns + a rook also attacking those same two pawns = 4 musts (queen×2 + rook×2)
Being in check = 1 must (king must move)
A rook with two capturable enemies within range = 2 musts
Example: King in check (1 must) + queen must capture a pawn (1 must) = 2 musts → loss, unless M1 applies.
Example: King in check (1 must) + queen attacks only a knight (0 musts, no obligation to capture knights) = 1 must total → no double-must loss.
Standard Checkmate
If a player has no legal moves and is in check, that is checkmate as in standard chess.
Bishop Checkmate
If it is your turn, your bishop must give check, but your king is in check so the king must move instead — that is checkmate, unless M1 applies.
Queen Checkmate
If it is your turn, your queen must capture a non-knight piece, but your king is in check so the king must move instead — that is checkmate, unless M1 applies.

Special Rules
M1 Special Rule
If a player has at least one must-obligation of any kind, any of that player's pieces may ignore all must-obligations for that move, provided the move satisfies both of the following:
The move is legal in standard chess — it cannot leave your own king in check. You cannot simply hang your king.
The move results in checkmate in 1 for the opponent under PDC rules.
When this applies, the player survives the must-violation and delivers checkmate instead of losing.
Key points:
The M1 move may come from any piece — not only the piece that has the must.
The move may be legal or illegal in PDC. Only standard chess legality is required.
The opponent is assumed to have no M1 response — this is a hypothetical assumption and is not verified.
There are no restrictions based on what kind of must is present, including king-move musts.

Draw & Win Conditions
2-Move Repetition Draw
There is no 3-fold repetition rule. If the same position occurs for the second time, a draw may be claimed before the repeated move is played — even if that move is forced.
Example: Kd6 Bf4+ Kd7 Bd2 Kd6 — after Kd6 appears for the second time, the draw may be claimed immediately.
If you are in a winning position, avoid repeating positions. If you have no alternative to a repeated move, the draw stands.
All other standard draw conditions apply: stalemate, insufficient material, and draw by agreement.
15-Move Space Win
If 15 consecutive moves pass with no captures, the player controlling more space on the opponent's half of the board wins immediately. This rule replaces the 50-move rule entirely.
Space is calculated as follows:
+1 per piece physically located on the opponent's half (default, always counts regardless of anything else).
+1 per square on the opponent's half reachable via ghost capture — the piece sweeps its PDC capture pattern and counts squares it could capture (empty or occupied). Empty squares always count. Occupied squares count only if that piece type is one the piece can actually capture under PDC rules. Any piece that would block the piece's movement still blocks it. Pins and check are ignored — space is always calculated as if it's your turn.
Per piece:
Pawn: the two forward diagonal squares. Empty squares count; an occupied square counts only if an enemy pawn is there (pawns may only capture pawns).
Rook: up to 3 squares per direction along files and ranks. Empty squares count; the first enemy piece encountered within 3 squares counts; any piece blocks further squares beyond it.
Knight: all 8 jump squares it can reach. Empty squares count; an occupied square counts only if that piece is currently giving check to your king (the only piece a knight can capture).
Bishop: all diagonal squares until the first piece. Empty squares count; any piece — own or enemy — blocks and does not itself count, since the bishop cannot capture anything.
Queen: all 8 directions until the first piece. Empty squares count; any enemy piece counts including knights (the queen can capture knights freely, it simply has no obligation to). Own pieces block.
King: adjacent empty squares only. Any piece — own or enemy — blocks and does not count, since the king cannot capture in PDC.
